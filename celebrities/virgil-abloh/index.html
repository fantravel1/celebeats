What were Virgil Abloh's favorite restaurants?

Virgil Abloh maintained deep connections to Chicago's food scene throughout his career. From his early days studying architecture at IIT to becoming a global fashion icon, he frequented everything from soul food spots to world-renowned restaurants like Alinea, always celebrating the creativity inherent in food.

Design & Dining Philosophy

Creative Vision

Virgil saw food and fashion as parallel creative disciplines. His approach to both reflected his philosophy of "the three percent rule" - that changing just 3% of something could make it new:

  • Creativity in Everything - Viewed dining as another form of artistic expression
  • Cultural Fusion - Appreciated how food, like fashion, bridges cultures
  • Chicago Pride - Always championed his hometown's food scene
  • Accessible Excellence - Loved both street food and fine dining equally

His legacy continues to inspire how we think about the intersection of design, culture, and culinary arts.

Frequently Asked Questions

Virgil Abloh frequented several Chicago establishments including Alinea for special occasions, casual spots in the West Loop, and various Chicago soul food restaurants that reminded him of his upbringing. He was also a fan of classic Chicago institutions like Lou Malnati's and Portillo's.

Virgil Abloh appreciated a wide range of cuisines but had a particular fondness for Chicago-style pizza, soul food from his Illinois roots, and high-end contemporary dining experiences at restaurants like Alinea. His Ghanaian heritage also influenced his appreciation for African-influenced dishes.

Virgil Abloh was born in Rockford, Illinois in 1980 and raised in the Chicago area. He studied architecture at the Illinois Institute of Technology, which heavily influenced his design aesthetic. His connection to Chicago remained strong throughout his career until his passing in 2021.

Virgil Abloh was a groundbreaking fashion designer, artist, and DJ. He founded the luxury streetwear brand Off-White and became Louis Vuitton's first Black artistic director for menswear. His work bridged high fashion and street culture, influencing a generation of designers.
