The Omakase Experience

What to Expect

  • Intimate counter seating (usually 8-12 seats)
  • 15-25 courses over 2-3 hours
  • Fish flown directly from Japan's Tsukiji Market
  • Each piece placed directly on your plate by the chef
  • Seasonal specialties and rare ingredients
  • Often includes o-toro, uni, A5 Wagyu, and caviar

The Art of Sushi

At celebrity-level omakase, you're witnessing decades of training. Sushi masters like Masa Takayama spent 10+ years just learning to prepare rice. The rice temperature, the precise fish cuts, the timing of each piece - everything is calibrated for perfection. You eat each piece within seconds of it being placed.

Rare Delicacies

  • O-Toro - Fatty tuna belly, the most prized cut
  • Hokkaido Uni - Sea urchin from cold waters
  • Kinmedai - Golden eye snapper
  • Nodoguro - Blackthroat sea perch
  • Shirako - Seasonal delicacy (winter only)

Omakase FAQs

Omakase means "I'll leave it up to you" in Japanese. It's a dining experience where the sushi chef creates a personalized multi-course meal based on the freshest available ingredients. At celebrity-level restaurants, this typically includes 15-25 courses of the finest sushi and sashimi.

Top celebrity omakase spots include Masa NYC (the most expensive restaurant in America at $750+), Sushi Ginza Onodera, Urasawa in Beverly Hills, n/naka in LA, and Nobu Malibu. These restaurants often have private rooms for A-listers seeking discretion.

Celebrity-frequented omakase ranges from $300-750 per person before drinks and gratuity. Masa NYC tops the list at $750, while spots like Urasawa and n/naka run $400-500. With sake pairings, a dinner for two can easily exceed $2,000.

Most elite omakase spots require booking 1-3 months in advance. Some use Resy or Tock and release reservations at midnight on specific days. Others only accept reservations by phone. Concierge services and hotel connections can help secure hard-to-get seats.
